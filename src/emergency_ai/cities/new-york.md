## Emergency numbers

- Police / Fire / Ambulance: **911**
- Poison Control: **1-800-222-1222**
- Suicide & Crisis Lifeline: **988**
- NYC Domestic Violence Hotline: **1-800-621-HOPE (4673)**
- 311 — non-emergency city services (noise, water, heat)

## Local laws relevant in emergencies

- **Good Samaritan Law (NY PHL §3000-a):** A person who voluntarily and without expectation of payment renders first aid in good faith is immune from civil liability for ordinary negligence.
- **Overdose Good Samaritan (NY PHL §220.78):** Calling 911 to report a suspected overdose grants the caller and the victim limited immunity from prosecution for small-quantity drug possession and possession of paraphernalia. Tell the dispatcher it is an overdose.
- **Mental health crisis — B-HEARD (NYC):** In parts of NYC, calling 911 for a mental health emergency may dispatch a B-HEARD social-worker + EMT team instead of police. You can request "no police, mental health response" but availability depends on the precinct and time of day.
- **Domestic violence:** Police have a mandatory arrest policy for primary aggressors when probable cause exists. Orders of protection are enforceable across the state.

## Practical notes

- FDNY responds to medical calls faster than NYPD in most boroughs. Saying "person not breathing" or "chest pains" routes to FDNY EMS.
- Subway emergency: pull the red emergency cord between cars only if the train must be stopped *now* (fire, person on tracks). Otherwise wait for the next station and notify the conductor — pulling the cord between stations strands the train mid-tunnel.
- Hospitals: closest Level 1 trauma centers — Bellevue (Manhattan), Lincoln (Bronx), Kings County (Brooklyn), Jamaica (Queens), Staten Island University.
- Heat emergencies: NYC opens cooling centers; 311 has the list. Risk groups: elderly without AC in walk-ups.
- Cold emergencies: Code Blue activates when feels-like temp drops below 32°F; outreach teams will transport unsheltered people to shelters even involuntarily.
