## Emergency numbers

- Police / Fire / Ambulance: **911**
- Poison Control: **1-800-222-1222**
- Suicide & Crisis Lifeline: **988**
- SF Mobile Crisis (mental health, non-police): **415-970-4000** (24/7)
- 311 — non-emergency city services

## Local laws relevant in emergencies

- **California Good Samaritan Law (Health & Safety Code §1799.102):** Provides immunity for both medical and non-medical care rendered in good faith at the scene of an emergency.
- **California 911 Good Samaritan Drug Law (HSC §11376.5):** Calling 911 for a drug overdose grants limited immunity for possession and being under the influence. The caller and victim are protected if they remain at the scene and cooperate.
- **5150 (W&I Code §5150):** Involuntary 72-hour psychiatric hold. Family members CANNOT directly initiate; only a designated officer or clinician can. For mental health crisis prefer Mobile Crisis (415-970-4000) over 911.
- **Earthquake response:** California Office of Emergency Services (Cal OES) coordinates. Drop-Cover-Hold-On indoors; do NOT run outside during shaking.

## Practical notes

- SFFD is fast for medical calls. For mental health, **Mobile Crisis Response Team** (call 415-970-4000) sends clinicians, not police — preferable when the person is non-violent.
- Hospitals: Zuckerberg SF General is the city's Level 1 trauma center (Mission/Potrero). UCSF Parnassus is large but not trauma.
- Earthquake (≥ M5): expect cellular congestion. Text messages and certain data apps may work when calls don't.
- Wildfire smoke: AirNow.gov AQI; >150 = unhealthy, mask up; >300 = avoid outdoors entirely.
- Fentanyl overdose is common. Narcan (naloxone) is free at most SF pharmacies and DPH sites without prescription.
