## Local laws relevant in emergencies

- **Ontario Good Samaritan Act** (Good Samaritan Act, RSO 1990, c G.8): A person who renders emergency medical services or first aid to an injured person at the immediate scene of an accident is not liable for damages that result from that person's negligence in acting or failing to act while rendering such services, unless it is gross negligence.
- **Ontario Drug and Pharmacies Regulation Act — Overdose Response** (Federal CDSA §4(2) + Ontario Naloxone Program): Ontario does not have a formal Good Samaritan drug law at provincial level, but the federal Good Samaritan Drug Overdose Act (2017) provides limited immunity from simple drug possession charges when calling 911 for an overdose. Naloxone is free at Ontario pharmacies under the Ontario Naloxone Program.

## Practical notes

- Toronto EMS, Toronto Fire, and Toronto Police all respond to 911. Medical calls are triaged to EMS (paramedics) with fire as first responder backup on life-threatening calls.
- TTC (subway) emergency: pull the passenger assistance alarm (PA alarm) in each car or use the yellow strip intercom on platforms. Operators stop at the next station to assess.
- Federal Good Samaritan Drug Overdose Act (2017): calling 911 for an overdose provides limited immunity from simple possession charges for the caller and others present — stay with the patient.
- Extreme cold warnings are issued at -20°C or below. Frostbite can occur within 10-15 minutes on exposed skin. Call 311 for Warming Centre locations.
- Toronto has the Toronto Overdose Prevention Society sites and supervised consumption sites — these reduce overdose fatalities.
- Naloxone (Narcan) nasal spray is free at any Ontario pharmacy — no prescription required under the Ontario Naloxone Program.
