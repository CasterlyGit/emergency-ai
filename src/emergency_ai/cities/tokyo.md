## Emergency numbers

- Fire / Ambulance: **119**
- Police: **110**
- Tokyo English Lifeline (mental health, English): **03-5774-0992**
- Japan Helpline (24/7 English, all emergencies): **0570-000-911**

## Local laws relevant in emergencies

- **Duty to render aid (Criminal Code Art. 218):** Japan has a general duty to render assistance to those whose lives are in danger when one is in a position to help. Failure can carry criminal liability, though prosecution is rare.
- **Good Samaritan protections:** No specific statutory immunity, but courts generally protect good-faith aid. The Japan Resuscitation Council and AED Foundation have lobbied for clearer protections; current practice is that civil suits against bystander rescuers are vanishingly rare.
- **AED access:** Public AEDs are common (stations, convenience stores, offices). Untrained civilians are explicitly authorized and encouraged to use them.
- **Foreign nationals:** No language barrier law — 119 has interpreters via three-way call, but it adds 30-60 seconds. Practice the Japanese phrase: "Kyūkyū desu" (救急です — "this is an emergency") to flag medical urgency.

## Practical notes

- **119 vs 110:** 119 = fire+ambulance, 110 = police. Calling the wrong number adds delay.
- Ambulances are **free** in Japan but discussed in the Diet for potential fee on non-urgent rides. Do not hesitate to call for a true emergency.
- In a crowded train: pull the emergency stop button (red, marked) only for immediate danger. For ill passenger, alert station staff at next stop — they have an AED and route to nearest hospital.
- **Earthquakes:** J-Alert / area-mail will buzz seconds before strong shaking. Drop-cover-hold. After shaking: open a door to prevent it jamming, then check gas.
- Hospitals: most don't take walk-in foreigners after hours. Ambulance is the path for night/weekend emergency care.
- English-speaking hospitals: St. Luke's International (Tsukiji), Tokyo Midtown Medical Center, Roppongi Hills Clinic.
